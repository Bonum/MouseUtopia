The Mouse Utopia Experiment—often called Universe 25—was a famous series of studies run in the 1960s–1970s by John B. Calhoun, a behavioral researcher. The experiment explored what happens when a population has unlimited food and shelter but limited space.

What Was the Setup?

A large enclosed habitat (“utopia”) for mice

Unlimited food and water

No predators or disease

Only constraint: finite physical space

Population allowed to grow naturally

What Happened? (The Four Phases)

1. Growth Phase
Started with 8 healthy mice → population exploded to ~2,200 (well below max capacity).

Population grew rapidly

Normal social behavior

2. Stagnation Phase

Overcrowding began

Social hierarchies broke down

3. Behavioral Sink

Severe behavioral changes:

Increased aggression

Mothers neglecting or attacking pups - infant cannibalism

Males withdrawing or becoming violent, hyper-aggression, failed parenting

Breakdown of mating behavior

A subgroup Calhoun called “the beautiful ones” emerged:
	Physically healthy
	Socially withdrawn
	No mating or parenting

4. Collapse & Extinction

Birth rates dropped to zero
Population aged and died out

Universe 25 ended in total extinction, despite abundant resources.
Despite endless resources, society broke down from overcrowding stress → birth rates crashed → total extinction by day ~600.

Even in paradise, space + social roles matter more than we think.

) The Experimental Conditions (What We’d Simulate)

To recreate Universe 25 in a simulation, you need to model the environment and the mouse behaviors precisely:

Environment

A closed enclosure with no predators, disease, or environmental stress.

Unlimited food and water via dispensers (e.g., 16 hoppers).

Ample nesting material and a temperature (~20 °C) ideal for mice.

Divided habitat with vertical and horizontal space (256 nesting boxes reachable by tunnels).

Initial population: 4 breeding pairs of young, healthy mice.

To simulate:
You must model space structure, resource access, and constraints on movement & territory.

📈 2) Population Growth Phases (Dynamics Over Time)

Calhoun described distinct population phases you’d want your simulation to reflect:

↗️ Phase A – Rapid Growth

After an adjustment period (~104 days), mice begin to reproduce.

Population doubles roughly every 55 days initially.

In a simulation:
Use logistic growth with resource saturation not being the limiting factor — space and social factors are.

⚖️ Phase B – Deceleration

As numbers approach ~600, growth slows to doubling every ~145 days.

Social roles begin to become rigid; stress starts affecting behavior.

Simulate by:
Increasing social stress factors as density rises — e.g., overcrowding, territorial disputes.

🌀 Phase C – Behavior Breakdown

This phase is where the real “interesting” dynamics occur — perfect for agent-based modelling.

Observed changes include:

• Behavioral Sink
Mice voluntarily cluster in busy areas (feeding zones) even when empty space remains elsewhere.

• Social Role Collapse

Some males become hyper-aggressive — attacking others with no clear goal.

Others become withdrawn and inactive.

Females reduce mating and maternal care; infant mortality may reach 90 %.

Some animals refuse all social interaction — dubbed “the beautiful ones.”

Simulation requirements:

Individual agents with states like: social, withdrawn, aggressive, nurturing, passive, etc.

Behavioral transitions based on density, stress, and social feedback.

Higher stress → increased aggression or withdrawal probabilities.

↘️ Phase D – Collapse / Extinction

Population peaks around ~2,200 (below theoretical max).

Birth rates drop sharply; social learning collapses.

Eventually zero births and all mice die off — even though resources are abundant.

In simulation:
Include feedback loops where social dysfunction reduces fertility, increases mortality, and ultimately leads to extinction.

🧠 3) Key Behavioral Mechanisms to Model

Here’s what drives the collapse and must be in your design:

🐁 Social Stress

Overcrowding creates stress not from lack of food, but from lack of meaningful roles and personal space, leading to:

aggression

passive withdrawing

failure to mate

maternal neglect

breakdown of hierarchy

Behavioral Sink = cluster-forming despite available space — a learned maladaptive behavior.

💥 Feedback Loops Needed

Your simulation should include:

Density → stress → abnormal behavior

Abnormal behavior → reduced reproduction

Reduced reproduction + infant mortality → population decline

Behavioral persistence over generations

This isn’t just biology — it’s social feedback.

🧩 4) Simulation Approaches
A. Agent-Based Model (ABM)

Each mouse as an agent:

Has internal states (stress, social rank, aggression)

Interacts with others and environment

Changes behavior based on proximity, density, social success

This is the closest to Universe 25 dynamics.

B. Population Dynamics + Behavior Rules

Combine:

Population growth model (birth/death rates)

Behavior state distribution (normal, aggressive, withdrawn)

Feedback from behavior to vital rates

This can be done with differential equations plus rule-based social dynamics.

📌 5) What You Won’t Simulate Easily

⛔ Genetic evolution — behaviors changed within a generation due to stress, not genetics.

Here’s a straightforward Python simulation plan for the Universe 25 mouse utopia experiment — focusing on agent-based modeling of mice in a constrained space with social behavior rules. This plan gives you a workable structure you can build on and extend.

🧠 1) Overview of the Simulation Concept

In Calhoun’s original Universe 25 experiment, mice had:

unlimited food and water,

no predators or disease,

plenty of nesting space,

but finite living area.

As the population grew, social breakdown occurred (behavioral sink) and ultimately the colony collapsed.

Your simulation will model:

individual mice as agents,

birth, death, and behavioral state transitions,

effects of crowding and social stress on reproduction and behavior.


📐 3) Structure the Simulation
A) Environment

Define a 2.5D grid or continuous 2D space:

Size corresponds to the physical size of the “utopia”.

There is no resource scarcity — food/water is always available.

B) Agents

Each mouse agent tracks:

Age

Sex (male/female)

Behavior state (e.g., normal, withdrawn, aggressive)

Reproductive status

C) Agent Lifecycle

At each timestep:

Movement — agent moves randomly or towards social interactions.

Social interaction — crowding increases social stress.

Birth & Death — females give birth based on a fertility function; agents die after age limit or social stress.

Behavior updates — high crowding may switch agents to temporary withdrawn or aggressive states.

🔁 4) Define Behavior Rules

Key dynamics to include:

⚖️ Birth Probability

Birth probability declines as crowding increases.

Example rule:

fertility = base_rate * max(0, 1 - crowding_factor)
🤝 Social Stress

Defined by local density around an agent.

High stress → greater chance of becoming withdrawn or aggressive.

Withdrawn agents don’t mate; aggressive agents may reduce births indirectly.

🧠 Behavioral Sink

Simulate agents clustering even if space is available — e.g., agents prefer to move toward groups.
